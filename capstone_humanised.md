# A STUDY ON CONSUMER BUYING BEHAVIOUR TOWARDS ONLINE SHOPPING PLATFORMS IN INDIA

## ABSTRACT

Online shopping has quietly become the default way many Indians buy things. Cheap data, a smartphone in almost every pocket, and the arrival of UPI have all played their part, and platforms like Amazon, Flipkart, Myntra, Meesho and Ajio now sit at the centre of everyday retail. The growth story is well known. What is less clear is why Indian shoppers pick one platform over another, how often they actually buy, what payment method they reach for, and which worries still hold them back.

This project looks at those questions using a dataset of 1,000 respondents spread across four age groups (18–24, 25–34, 35–44 and 45+), three city tiers (Metro, Tier 2, Tier 3) and both genders. The analysis is mostly descriptive — frequency tables, cross-tabs, and charts built around the ten variables in the dataset.

A few things stood out. Amazon comes out on top overall (21.6%), but only just, with Myntra close behind and stronger among female shoppers. Most people shop "occasionally" (35.7%) rather than weekly, which tells me habitual online shopping still has room to grow. Friends and family remain the biggest influence on purchase decisions (27.9%), slightly ahead of advertisements (25.8%). On payments, credit and debit cards together lead, but UPI is right behind at 23.1% — a big change from even five years ago. Privacy (22.4%) was the single most cited concern, followed closely by product quality (20.4%).

Overall, the data supports the idea that age, gender and city tier do shape online shopping behaviour in India, and that trust-related concerns still weigh heavily on consumers. The last chapter talks about what this means for platforms — mainly around trust, personalisation, Tier 2/3 expansion, and payment security.

**Keywords:** Consumer Buying Behaviour, Online Shopping, India, E-Commerce, Platform Preference, Purchase Frequency, Digital Payments, Consumer Concerns, Demographic Analysis

## CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Background of the Study

India's e-commerce market has grown at a pace that few other sectors have matched. It was worth roughly USD 83 billion in 2023 and is expected to cross USD 350 billion by 2030. A mix of factors is driving this — more than 900 million internet users, cheap smartphones, and a payments stack built around UPI. Programmes like Digital India and Jan Dhan Yojana have pulled a lot of first-time digital users online, and many of them live in Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities rather than the metros.

Retail itself has changed shape in the process. Kirana stores and malls still matter, but a growing share of spending moves through online platforms because they are simply easier — one app, a wider catalogue, and price comparison without having to walk anywhere. Amazon India, Flipkart, Myntra, Meesho and Ajio have all poured money into warehousing, seller onboarding and delivery to take advantage of this shift.

Online shopping is not a straight copy of the offline experience, though. You cannot touch the product, you rely on reviews written by strangers, you pick from several payment options, and you wait for a delivery that may or may not arrive on time. How Indian consumers deal with all that — why they trust one platform more than another, what puts them off, and how demographic background shapes their choices — is the question this study is trying to understand.

### 1.2 Research Problem

Despite how much has been written about e-commerce globally, the picture for India specifically is still patchy. A lot of existing work comes from Western markets, or focuses on one narrow variable at a time. What seems to be missing is a single study that puts platform preference, purchase frequency, influence factors, payment choices, category mix and consumer concerns side by side and looks at them together, across Indian demographic segments.

The core question here is: **what are the main drivers of consumer buying behaviour on online shopping platforms in India, and do age, gender and city tier change the pattern?**

### 1.3 Scope of the Study

The study looks at consumer behaviour across five major Indian platforms — Amazon, Flipkart, Myntra, Meesho and Ajio. Within that, it covers:

- Platform preference across age, gender and city tier
- How often people shop online, and who shops more frequently
- What influences the purchase decision — social media, ads, influencers, or peers
- Payment preferences, including UPI, cards and Cash on Delivery
- The product categories being bought — fashion, electronics, grocery, beauty and others
- The concerns that keep coming up — privacy, quality, payment fraud, fake reviews and delivery delays

The sample has 1,000 respondents spread across four age groups and three city tiers. The dataset is broad but it is still a sample, so the findings should be read in that light rather than treated as the full picture of Indian consumers.

### 1.4 Objectives of the Study

The study is built around the following objectives:

- To understand which platforms Indian shoppers prefer and how that varies by demographic
- To look at how often people shop online and link that to age, gender and city tier
- To identify what actually pushes a consumer to complete a purchase
- To check how far digital payments have replaced Cash on Delivery
- To see which categories are pulling the most online purchases
- To rank the concerns that bother Indian shoppers the most
- To pull all of this into a set of practical recommendations for e-commerce platforms

### 1.5 Need and Relevance of the Study

There are a few reasons why this question is worth looking at right now. India has more than 650 million people under 35, and their shopping habits are still taking shape. For any e-commerce business, getting this group right is not optional — it directly feeds into product decisions, where marketing spend goes, how the app is designed, and what keeps users coming back.

From an academic angle, consumer behaviour in digital settings is still a moving target. Models like the Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB), the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and UTAUT give us a starting point, but they were built mostly in Western, relatively uniform markets. India is neither — income levels, digital literacy and urban/rural differences vary enormously, so these models need to be checked against Indian data rather than assumed to apply.

On top of that, newer formats — social commerce, influencer marketing, quick commerce — didn't even exist when most of the foundational consumer behaviour theories were written. This study tries to bring some of that more recent context into the picture.

### 1.6 Hypotheses

The study tests the following hypotheses:

- **H₁:** Platform preference among Indian online shoppers varies significantly by age, gender and city tier.
- **H₂:** Purchase frequency depends on the consumer's demographic profile, especially age group and city tier.
- **H₃:** Social media and peer recommendations are the dominant influence factors for younger shoppers (18–34).
- **H₄:** Digital payment methods (UPI, credit cards) are now used more than Cash on Delivery, at least in urban areas.
- **H₅:** Privacy and quality concerns are the biggest obstacles to online shopping adoption and retention in India.

### 1.7 Organisation of the Report

The report runs across five chapters. Chapter 1 sets up the background, problem, objectives and hypotheses. Chapter 2 reviews the existing literature on consumer behaviour, e-commerce adoption and Indian online retail. Chapter 3 explains how the data was collected and analysed. Chapter 4 is the main empirical section, with tables and charts for each variable. Chapter 5 pulls the findings together, talks about what they mean for platforms, and closes with some directions for future work.

## CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW

### 2.1 Introduction to the Literature Review

This chapter looks at what existing research says about consumer behaviour in e-commerce, with a tilt towards studies that have something to say about India. It is arranged by theme rather than by date — starting with the theoretical models, then moving on to studies on platform preference, influence factors, payments and consumer concerns.

### 2.2 Theoretical Foundations of Consumer Buying Behaviour

Consumer behaviour sits at the intersection of economics, psychology, sociology and marketing, so there is no shortage of theory to draw from. The older models, such as the Engel-Blackwell-Miniard (EBM) framework, describe the buying decision as a set of stages: you recognise a need, search for information, evaluate options, buy, and then reflect on the purchase. Online shopping does not kill this sequence, but it does squash it. Reviews, comparison tools and one-click buying all compress stages that used to take days.

Davis (1989) introduced the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), which argues that whether people adopt a technology comes down to two things: whether they think it is useful, and whether they think it is easy to use. Applied to e-commerce, the logic is straightforward — if the app is easy to navigate and genuinely helps people find or compare products, they keep coming back. Venkatesh et al. (2003) later extended this into UTAUT, which adds social influence and facilitating conditions (for example, having a stable internet connection or a working payment method) as further predictors.

Ajzen (1991) proposed the Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB), which puts attitudes, subjective norms and perceived behavioural control at the centre of intention. In an online shopping context, this translates into three things: how the consumer feels about buying online, what the people around them think about it, and how confident they feel navigating the platform.

### 2.3 E-Commerce Adoption in India: Key Studies

A number of studies have looked at e-commerce adoption in India specifically. Dholakia and Uusitalo (2002) pointed out that emerging markets do not follow the same online-offline separation that developed markets do. Indian shoppers tend to mix the two — researching online before buying in a shop ("webrooming") or browsing in a shop before ordering online ("showrooming"). This hybrid behaviour is still very visible today.

Sharma and Mittal (2009) ran one of the earlier studies on Indian online shopping. They found that convenience, lower prices, and access to a wider range of products were the main reasons people shopped online. On the other side, worries about security, product quality and after-sales service were what held people back. Their findings are in line with global studies, but with one Indian difference: trust mattered more, because digital literacy was still developing.

KPMG India (2021) argued that the COVID-19 pandemic was a turning point. Lockdowns kept people out of shops, and platforms picked up a lot of first-time users, particularly from Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities. Around the same time, UPI adoption accelerated — helped by government promotion of BHIM and the rise of Google Pay, PhonePe and Paytm.

### 2.4 Platform Preferences and Competitive Dynamics

The Indian e-commerce market is competitive in a way that most other markets are not. Amazon and Flipkart fight it out in the broad "everything store" segment, mostly on delivery speed, selection and price. Myntra and Ajio focus on fashion, leaning on curated brand collections and lifestyle content. Meesho has carved out its own space by targeting value-conscious shoppers in smaller cities, using a social-commerce model where resellers push products through WhatsApp.

Research on platform loyalty in e-commerce (Chiu et al., 2009; Ribbink et al., 2004) identifies perceived value, service quality and trust as the main predictors of repeat use. In India, price sensitivity is especially sharp, which is why flash sales like Flipkart's Big Billion Days and Amazon's Great Indian Festival have become such fixtures on the retail calendar.

### 2.5 Influence Factors in Online Purchase Decisions

The influence of social networks and peer recommendations on purchase behaviour is well documented. Cheung and Lee (2012) showed that electronic word-of-mouth — essentially online reviews — has a real effect on purchase intention, and the effect is strongest in categories like electronics where the stakes of buying wrong are higher. In India, family and friends still carry a lot of weight as recommendation sources, even as digital channels expand.

Influencer marketing has also become a noticeable part of the mix. Instagram, YouTube and ShareChat have built large ecosystems of creators with loyal audiences, which brands now tap into routinely. Mediakix (2022) estimated the Indian influencer marketing industry at around USD 375 million, with fashion, beauty and lifestyle being the biggest segments.

### 2.6 Payment Behaviour and Digital Finance

UPI, launched in 2016, has probably done more to change Indian payment behaviour than any other single development. UPI transactions hit 117.6 billion in FY 2024. RBI (2023) noted that UPI adoption is especially strong in the 18–35 age group and in urban areas. Cash on Delivery, though, has not disappeared — it is still widely used in Tier 3 cities and among older consumers, where trust and digital literacy remain the main friction points.

### 2.7 Consumer Concerns and Barriers

Pavlou and Fygenson (2006) flagged privacy and security as some of the biggest blockers to e-commerce adoption. In India, the list is a bit longer — payment fraud, fake reviews, counterfeit products and late deliveries all show up regularly. IAMAI (2023) reported that around 35% of surveyed consumers had received a product that did not match its online description. That "quality gap" between the listing and the product is a persistent problem for horizontal marketplaces that rely on third-party sellers.

### 2.8 Research Gaps and Study Contribution

Reading through the existing work, a few gaps stand out. Most older Indian studies predate UPI and the pandemic-era shift, so they do not fully reflect today's environment. Very few look at all five major Indian platforms in a single framework. And city tier, even though it clearly matters for platform strategy, has not been examined as a moderating variable as often as age or gender. This study is an attempt to fill those gaps by looking at consumer behaviour across India's leading platforms, across multiple behavioural dimensions, with data that is recent.

## CHAPTER 3: RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

### 3.1 Research Design

The study uses a quantitative, descriptive design. The goal is to describe what Indian online shoppers look like across a set of behavioural and demographic variables, not to test a causal model. A cross-sectional survey was used, with responses collected through a structured questionnaire from 1,000 respondents across multiple demographic segments.

### 3.2 Population and Sample

The target population is Indian consumers who have shopped on at least one of the five major platforms (Amazon, Flipkart, Myntra, Meesho, Ajio) in the last six months. A stratified random sampling approach was used so that the sample would cover all four age groups (18–24, 25–34, 35–44, 45+), all three city tiers (Metro, Tier 2, Tier 3), and both genders.

The final dataset has 1,000 valid responses. The intent was to capture both high-frequency urban shoppers and more occasional users from smaller cities, so neither end of the market is ignored.

### 3.3 Dataset Variables

The dataset includes ten variables. Table 3.1 describes each one.

|---|---|---|
| Age_Group | Categorical | 18–24, 25–34, 35–44, 45+ |
| City_Tier | Categorical | Metro, Tier 2, Tier 3 |
| Gender | Categorical | Male, Female |
| Preferred_Platform | Categorical | Amazon, Flipkart, Myntra, Meesho, Ajio |
| Monthly_Online_Spend | Continuous (₹) | ₹501 – ₹10,000 (Mean: ₹5,417) |
| Purchase_Frequency | Ordinal | Weekly, Occasionally, Monthly |
| Influenced_By | Categorical | Friends/Family, Ads, Social Media, Influencers |
| Payment_Method | Categorical | Credit Card, Debit Card, COD, UPI |

*Table 3.1: Dataset Variables Description*

### 3.4 Analytical Methods

The analysis uses the following methods:

- **Frequency distribution analysis:** absolute and relative frequencies for every categorical variable.
- **Descriptive statistics:** mean, median, standard deviation and quartiles for Monthly_Online_Spend.
- **Cross-tabulation:** contingency tables to see how demographic variables (age, gender, city tier) line up with behavioural ones (platform preference, payment method, purchase frequency).
- **Graphical visualisation:** bar charts, pie charts, histograms and grouped bar charts, built to make the patterns easier to read at a glance.

### 3.5 Limitations of the Study

The study has its limits, and it is worth being honest about them. First, the data is cross-sectional — it is a snapshot, not a trend line, so it cannot say how behaviour is shifting over time. Second, rural consumers are not included, even though rural e-commerce is growing fast. Third, the data is self-reported, which means some amount of social desirability bias is probably baked in — people may overstate how often they shop or how comfortable they are with digital payments. Fourth, only five platforms are covered; niche players like Nykaa, Tata Cliq and JioMart are left out.

## CHAPTER 5: FINDINGS, IMPLICATIONS, SUGGESTIONS AND CONCLUSION

### 5.1 Key Findings

Pulling together the analysis of 1,000 responses across ten variables, the main findings are as follows:

**Finding 1: Competitive Platform Landscape**
There is no clear winner. Amazon leads at 21.6%, but Myntra (20.9%), Flipkart (19.5%), Ajio (19.3%) and Meesho (18.7%) are all clustered right behind. The market is close enough that the platform a consumer picks depends more on category, price or a friend's recommendation than on brand loyalty.

**Finding 2: Gender Shapes Platform Selection**
Women lean towards Myntra (126 vs. 83) and Meesho (102 vs. 85). Men lean towards Amazon (124 vs. 92) and Ajio (105 vs. 88). These are not marginal differences — they reflect how each platform's catalogue and positioning maps onto gendered buying preferences.

**Finding 3: Occasional Shopping Dominates**
35.7% of respondents shop only occasionally; weekly and monthly shoppers make up 33.7% and 30.6%. In other words, there is real room to move occasional users up the frequency ladder, which is exactly what loyalty programs and subscriptions are built for.

**Finding 4: Trust in Peer Recommendations**
Friends and family (27.9%) beat every digital channel as a purchase trigger. Social media (24.0%) and influencers (22.3%) matter, and ads (25.8%) are still doing their job — but people still trust people they know more than anything else.

**Finding 5: Digital Payment Maturation**
Combined digital payments (credit + debit + UPI) come to 75.5%, well above Cash on Delivery at 24.5%. Digital has clearly won in aggregate. That said, 24.5% COD is not a small tail — there is still a meaningful group of consumers who prefer to pay only after they have the product in their hands.

**Finding 6: Privacy as the Paramount Concern**
Privacy leads at 22.4%, narrowly ahead of quality (20.4%), payment fraud (19.7%), fake reviews (19.0%) and delivery delay (18.5%). What matters here is not just the ranking but how bunched together the five concerns are — platforms cannot solve one and ignore the rest.

**Finding 7: Fashion Emerging as Top Category**
Fashion (20.9%) is essentially tied with Others (21.0%) at the top. Electronics (19.8%), beauty (19.2%) and grocery (19.1%) follow closely. The even spread across categories suggests horizontal platforms are doing a decent job covering most needs, while fashion has clearly grown into its own as a standalone segment.

**Finding 8: Mid-Range Spending Cluster**
With a mean monthly spend of ₹5,417 and a median of ₹5,565, most shoppers sit in the ₹3,000–₹8,000 range. A standard deviation of ₹2,759 means there is enough variation for platforms to work with, whether through upselling, bundles or premium membership.

### 5.2 Hypothesis Validation

Looking back at the five hypotheses set up in Chapter 1:

- **H₁ Supported:** Platform preference does vary by gender (clearly) and by age (more subtly). Younger shoppers lean harder towards Myntra and Ajio.
- **H₂ Partially Supported:** Purchase frequency varies with age, with weekly shopping more common in the 18–34 range, but the relationship is not as sharp as expected.
- **H₃ Partially Supported:** Social media and influencers do matter more for younger consumers, but friends and family still sit on top for everyone.
- **H₄ Supported:** UPI and cards together clearly outweigh COD at 75.5% to 24.5%.
- **H₅ Supported:** Privacy and quality are the top two concerns, though the spread across all five makes it clear this is a multi-dimensional trust problem, not a single-issue one.

### 5.3 Practical Implications for E-Commerce Platforms

A few practical implications come out of the findings:

**Implication 1: Gender-Targeted Product Curation**
Platforms should not treat discovery as a one-size-fits-all problem. Fashion platforms can lean harder into women-focused curation; horizontal platforms can build stronger electronics and tech buying flows for their male base. The data clearly supports this kind of split.

**Implication 2: Tier 2 and Tier 3 Market Penetration**
Between them, Tier 2 and Tier 3 account for 69.6% of the sample. That is not a future opportunity — it is already the main market. Regional language support, reliable last-mile delivery and well-handled COD are what will keep these users coming back.

**Implication 3: Trust Architecture Investment**
With privacy, fake reviews and payment fraud all sitting near the top of the concern list, trust needs to be treated as an engineering problem, not a marketing one. Better seller verification, AI-based review filtering, clearer privacy policies and tokenised payments with multi-factor authentication are the building blocks.

**Implication 4: Social Commerce Integration**
Friends and family (27.9%) plus social media (24.0%) together cover more than half of all purchase influence. That is a strong case for features like shareable wishlists, group buying and referral rewards — basically, letting organic social influence do more of the work inside the platform itself.

**Implication 5: Purchase Frequency Enhancement**
35.7% of users shopping only occasionally is a big bucket to work with. Subscription programs like Amazon Prime and Flipkart Plus are the obvious lever, but personalised push notifications and reminders tied to past purchases can also help move this group up the frequency curve.

### 5.4 Suggestions for Industry Stakeholders

Some specific suggestions for platforms operating in India:

- Put real investment into personalisation — not just "customers who bought X also bought Y," but recommendations that adjust in real time to behaviour, purchase history and demographic signals.
- Publish transparent data-handling policies, with clear opt-ins and disclosures. Privacy is the top concern in this data, and platforms should treat it that way.
- Strengthen seller certification and product authenticity checks. Fake reviews and counterfeit products are still eroding trust faster than marketing can rebuild it.
- Build regional-language apps and voice-first interfaces to reach Tier 2/3 users who may not be comfortable with English-heavy UIs.
- Work with fintech partners to push UPI adoption through cashback, zero-cost EMI and similar incentives. This helps move the remaining COD share over to digital.
- Treat influencer partnerships as long-term, disclosed relationships rather than one-off campaigns. Authenticity matters more than reach.

### 5.5 Conclusion

This capstone has looked at consumer buying behaviour on online shopping platforms in India using primary data from 1,000 consumers across different age groups, city tiers and genders. What the data shows is a market that is genuinely competitive, where no platform has a commanding lead, where trust is still a live issue, and where gender and city tier make a real difference in how people shop.

Indian e-commerce is no longer in its early adoption phase. It has moved into a stage where platforms have to compete on more than just price — personalisation, trust, content quality, and what happens after the product arrives all matter now. The prominence of privacy and quality concerns, alongside how much weight friends and family still carry in purchase decisions, points to the same underlying theme: consumers want to feel like the platform is on their side.

This study adds to the existing literature by bringing five major Indian platforms and ten behavioural dimensions into a single, contemporary analysis. There is plenty of room for follow-up work. Longitudinal data would help track how these patterns shift over time, and qualitative interviews would fill in the "why" behind the numbers reported here.
